How Self-Control Affects Perceived Power and Leader Emergence

Wu, S., Smallman, R. & Smith, P.K. (2024, January 25). Self-Control Signals and Affords Power. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology. Advance online publication. https://dx.doi.org/10.1037/pspi0000457.[PDF]

Media Coverage: UCSD Today, Newsweek
Whom do we perceive as more powerful and prefer to give power to: Those who have self-control or those who lack it? Past theory and research provide divergent predictions. Low self-control can be seen as a form of disinhibition, and disinhibition has been associated with greater power. However, high self-control can be seen as a form of agency, which is associated with greater power. Across seven studies, we found that individuals who exhibited high self-control were seen as more powerful, and given more power, than individuals who exhibited low self-control. We also demonstrated an important implication of our findings for goal setting: People were perceived as more powerful and given more power when they had a modest goal but exceeded it than when they had an ambitious goal but failed to meet it, even though in both cases they performed the same action. A meta-analysis of our mediation results showed that people perceived individuals higher in self-control as more assertive and competent, which was associated with greater power perception and then with greater power conferral. Perceived competence also directly mediated the effect of self-control on power conferral. The current research addresses a theoretical debate in the power literature and contributes to a better understanding of how power is perceived and accrued.

Lay Theories of Power-Morality Link

Wu, S., & Smith, P.K. Lay Theories about the Effect of Power on Morality and Their Organizational Implications [title redacted]. Under review at Journal of Applied Psychology.

Keywords: lay theories, power, morality, trust, organizational policy
• Obtained a research grant exceeding $15,000 to investigate lay theories concerning the causal impact of power on morality and their organizational implications.
• Applied qualitative coding techniques to analyze lay theories related to the effect of power on morality, revealing two prominent themes: "Power Corrupts" and "Power Reveals".
• Developed and validated a two-factor scale to measure the lay theories, conducting rigorous tests for factor structure, measurement invarance, test-retest reliability, and discriminant validity.
• Demonstrated the incremental validity of the lay theories in predicting motivation to attain power at work using hierarchical linear regressions and structural equation modeling.
• Conducted a time-lagged field survey to examine the differential impact of the two lay theories on trust towards top management and supervisors.
• Recruited representative samples to investigate the varying effects of the two lay theories on support for organizational policies aimed at mitigating power abuse.
• Organized and chaired a symposium on "Power, Status, and Leadership" at the Academy of Management (AOM) conference to present research findings.
• Drafted a research paper as the first author for submission to Journal of Applied Psychology, highlighting the impact of lay theories in the workplace.

Self-Disclosure in Unequal Power Dyads

Wu, S., Yin, Y., Smith, P.K., Oveis, C., & Ocampo, J. Power and self-disclosure [title redacted]. Revising to be submitted to Organization Science.

Keywords: power, self-disclosure, trust, social connection, dyadic interaction
• Led a team of 5 researchers and supervised 20+ research assistants to investigate 1) the impact of self-disclosure depth on trust in unequal-power dyads and 2) the effect of social power on the depth of self-disclosure.
• Developed, preregistered, and implemented a Zoom-based dyadic experiment involving over 600 participants and analyzed conversation content to reveal a significant finding: individuals in positions of power exhibit reduced self-disclosure depth.
• Employed a combination of vigenette-based experiments and field surveys to uncover why high-power individuals self-disclose less deeply: they perceive less value in social connection.
• Disseminated research findings by delivering presentations at conferences and contributed in writing and enhancing the manuscript as a co-first author.

A Status Signaling Account of Maximizing

Wu, S., & Luan, M. A status signaling account of maximizing [title redacted]. Under review at Journal of Marketing Research.

Keywords: maximizing, status, signaling, personal branding, social influence
• Co-drafted a research proposal that secured funding exceeding $40,000 and published the proposal in Advances in Psychological Science.
• Developed a novel Linguistic Inquiry and Word Count (LIWC) dictionary to measure maximizing mindset from text and utilized the bag-of-words approach to study the relationship between maximizing language and social influence in a large recipe dataset containing over 180,000 recipes.
• Conducted more than 5 experiments to empirically demonstrate the effect of maximizing (vs. non-maximizing) on enhancing perceived status and, subsequently, social influence, highlighting its implications for marketing strategies and personal branding.
• Utilized mediation and moderation analyses to investigate the underlying mechanism, revealing that maximizing serves as a signal of agency.
• Presented research findings at the Association for Psychological Science (APS) conference and authored a working paper intended for submission to Journal of Consumer Research.
